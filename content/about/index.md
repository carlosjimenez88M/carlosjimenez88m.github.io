## About

I grew up in Colombia between two things that don't obviously belong together: numbers and music. My father was an economist; the house was always full of vallenato, Carlos Gardel, and the occasional Beatles record. I ended up making a career at the intersection of both — using quantitative tools to understand systems, and occasionally turning those same tools on the music itself.

My professional work is in MLOps and AI systems engineering. The specific problem I care about is the gap between a model that works in a notebook and one that works reliably in production. That gap is wider than most people admit, and closing it requires a kind of thinking that most ML education doesn't teach: operational discipline, systems design, and a tolerance for the unglamorous work of making things actually run.

Before going deep on infrastructure, I worked at the United Nations and the Inter-American Development Bank on data and policy problems in Latin America. Then research at Yale University. Then engineering roles at Mercado Libre and Globant. I've taught ML at universities in Colombia, Mexico, Chile, and Spain. Those experiences left me with a strong prior that the interesting problems are rarely purely technical — they are technical *and* organizational *and* contextual, all at once.

My current technical focus is on three things: **LLMOps** (the next iteration of MLOps, shaped by the requirements of large language models in production), **AI software engineering** (the systems layer around model training and inference — hardware trade-offs, serving architecture, cost), and **GCP** (where most of my production work runs). I write about all of this here, trying to be specific enough to be useful and honest about what I don't know.

I also analyze music computationally. I'm a vinyl collector and a serious listener, and the question of what NLP and LLM tools can and cannot tell us about musical structure is one I find genuinely worth investigating. The short answer: less than you'd hope, and the failures are the interesting part.

Outside of work: I study economic history — specifically, religion's influence on Latin American economic development and the emergence of political messianism in the region. I collect books the way some people collect records.
