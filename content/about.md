## The path here

Throughout my journey in AI, I've had the privilege of working on problems that actually matter—from detecting fraud patterns at Mercado Libre to evaluating social development projects across Latin America at the Inter-American Development Bank.

Currently **Principal MLOps Engineer at Globant**, where I design ML pipelines and AI solutions for clients in the UAE and United States. My day-to-day: recommendation systems, LLM-powered applications, computer vision, chatbots, and making sure models don't just work in notebooks.

Before Globant, I was **AI Engineer at Yale University**, collaborating with NVIDIA on edge computing research—optimizing memory usage, energy consumption, and model weights for constrained devices. That project solidified something I already suspected: the future of ML isn't just in the cloud.

At **Mercado Libre**, I led a team building pricing models for Argentina, Brazil, and Mexico. When your Bayesian model affects millions of dynamic auctions, you learn to care deeply about uncertainty quantification.

Earlier: **ML Engineer at the Inter-American Development Bank** (AI for policy evaluation), **Data Scientist at the United Nations** in Chile (criminal detection models), and five years at Bogotá's Education Department building the first AI system that automatically wrote institutional reports.

## What I care about

**Agentic AI** — Systems that don't just predict, but act. I'm interested in how we build AI that makes autonomous decisions, and the infrastructure required to deploy and monitor it reliably.

**Edge Computing** — This is where I experiment. I build computer vision models on Raspberry Pi and Jetson Nano, optimizing for memory and energy. It's ML where cloud isn't an option.

## The longer story

Before I wrote a single line of Python, I spent years studying economics, philosophy, and history. Not as parallel hobbies—as a single obsession with a recurring question: why do some societies develop the way they do, and others don't?

That question pulled me toward two research threads. The first: the role of religion in economic development—how theological doctrine, institutional church structures, and belief systems shaped the material conditions of entire regions. The second: the doctrines of power in Latin America—the messianic and caudillista traditions that gave political authority a quasi-sacred character, and how those traditions persist inside modern institutions.

At some point I realized that to answer these questions seriously, I needed better tools. Not better archives—better methods. That's what pushed me into statistics and computer science. Not as a career pivot, but as a natural extension: the same curiosity that made me want to understand incentive structures in colonial economies eventually made me want to model uncertainty, build simulations, and interrogate data at scale.

From there the path was less surprising than it looks from the outside. Statistical inference led to machine learning. Machine learning led to thinking about deployment—what happens when a model has to make decisions in production, under real conditions, without a data scientist hovering over it. That's MLOps. And MLOps, once you take it seriously, leads to agents: systems that don't just predict but act, reason, and coordinate.

Now I spend a lot of my time thinking about how agents communicate—not just how a single model makes decisions, but how information flows between agents, how they negotiate shared context, and what it means to build reliable pipelines where the units of computation are autonomous systems talking to each other.

The thread connecting all of it is the same question I started with: why do systems behave the way they do?
